GC Next — "New" Indicator Options

Visual exploration for labeling new features on individual items within the product map. Shown in context across functional area tiles and list items.

Option A
Filled pill badge
A compact, fully filled badge — readable and familiar. Works well inline next to text. Color variants let you choose a neutral or a brand accent. The current "NEW" tag on Celebrate uses a looser version of this idea but floated/absolute-positioned on a colored header.

Option B
Outlined pill badge
Same shape as A but open/ghost style — lighter visual weight. Feels less like an alert, more like a label. Reads well on white card backgrounds. Could feel subtle enough to pair with colored tile backgrounds.

Option C
Corner ribbon (tiles only)
A classic "new" treatment from e-commerce/app stores. Works exclusively on tile-shaped elements — the small triangular banner pulls the eye immediately. Not applicable to inline list items without awkward overflow. Best for functional area tiles.

Note

Ribbons are visually punchy but only work on rectangular tiles with overflow:hidden. Not suitable for list items or inline text. Consider pairing with a different treatment for list-level items if you use this for tiles.

Option D
Pulse dot
An animated dot with a soft ripple — subtle motion draws attention without adding text. Works well for items where you want the "new" signal to be felt rather than read. Can feel app-like and modern. The animation can be turned off if the map is exported or printed.

Option E
Left accent bar
A flush left border stripe. Very space-efficient — adds no horizontal width to tiles, no inline text clutter on list items. Communicates "this row is different" cleanly. Works for both tiles and list items but may be too subtle as a standalone signal; usually pairs with another indicator.

Option F
Highlighted background
The whole tile or row item gets a tinted background and subtle border. Works well if you want "new" to be visually grouped and easy to scan at a glance — the eye reads the colored region, not a small badge. Can feel a bit loud if many items are new.

Option G
Icon / emoji prefix
A small icon or emoji before the label carries the "new" signal through symbol rather than text. ✦ (black four-pointed star) or ⊕ convey "added" and "new" without reading "NEW". Can feel fresh and modern. Risk: may not scan as clearly as text to all viewers.

Option H
Superscript dot or star
An unobtrusive raised dot (•) or star (✦) after the label — minimal visual interruption. The least disruptive option; the viewer only notices on close reading. Works well if you want the map to look clean from a distance but reveal detail on inspection.

Option I
Stacked "New" sub-label
The word "NEW" appears below the item label as a second line — no badge shape, just colored type. Keeps the tile text clean while making the label explicit. Matches the typographic character of the diagram rather than adding a foreign UI element.

Option J
Tonal chip (no outline)
A filled badge but with a light, desaturated background and matching text color — the "tonal" chip pattern from Material Design. Reads clearly as a badge without the visual weight of a full-color fill. Very readable on both white card backgrounds and colored tiles. Recommended as a strong baseline candidate.

Combination
Suggested pairing: J (tonal chip) + D (pulse dot)
Tonal chip for tiles and inline list items — readable, space-efficient, low-noise. Pulse dot for feature areas that are especially high-emphasis or where motion helps (Celebrate, Grow). The two can coexist without visual conflict because they occupy different semantic roles.

Pillar-level effects
Pulsing treatments for new & elevated pillars
These effects apply to the pillar header card itself — Celebrate (New) and Grow (Elevated). Each row shows all four pillars side by side so you can judge how the marked ones read against the unmarked ones. Organize and Connect are shown at rest as reference. Celebrate and Grow carry the pulse treatment.
Option K
Shadow breathe
The pillar's existing colored box-shadow slowly expands and contracts — like the card is breathing. Uses the pillar's own accent color so it reads as "alive" rather than jarring. The subtlest pillar-level motion; barely perceptible at rest but noticeable on the page.

Option L
Expanding ring (sonar)
A ring expands outward from the pillar card edge and fades to nothing — like a sonar ping. Draws the eye strongly on first load, then repeats at a slow cadence. More active than the shadow breathe; good for diagrams where these pillars need to read as "launch" items.

Gradient border pulse

Option N
Shimmer sweep
A highlight band sweeps across the card from left to right, like light catching a glossy surface. Feels polished and product-quality — closer to how app stores or dashboards signal "new" on premium content. More stylized than the other options; may feel overly app-like for an architecture document.

Option P
Double ring cascade
Two concentric rings expand outward in sequence — a 0.4s offset between them creates a cascade effect, like ripples from a stone in water. More dramatic than a single ring. Strong first-load impression; works well if these two pillars are genuinely the most important story on the diagram.

Option Q
Combined: shadow breathe + tonal chip on sub-items
The pillar header breathes (Option K) while the items inside it carry tonal chips (Option J). Two levels of signal — the header tells you "this whole pillar is new," the chips tell you "this specific feature is new." The two signals are complementary, not competing.
